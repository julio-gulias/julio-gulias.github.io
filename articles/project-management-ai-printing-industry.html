Project Management and AI: What a Printing Industry Revolution Can Teach Us.

The printing industry didn't just change tools — it changed what was expected of everyone in it. AI may be doing the same thing to project management.

AI Project Management Future of Work Digital Transformation Change Management

Originally published on LinkedIn on July 3, 2026

AI may not replace project managers.

But it will almost certainly change what organizations expect from them.

And this would not be the first time a technology revolution has reshaped an entire professional landscape.

Around 30 years ago, I experienced something similar in the printing industry.

This article is not an attempt to predict the future. It is a reflection based on a technological transformation I saw closely: the shift from a highly manual, specialized, and sequential graphic production process to a much more digital, integrated, and faster one.

I am not covering here the later impact of the internet on the printing industry. That would be a different discussion.

My focus is on a specific moment: when technology started to combine steps, reduce manual dependencies, and reshape professional roles.

The old printing process

For many years, the print production cycle was long, expensive, and highly specialized.

A printed piece, such as a brochure, catalogue, advertisement, package, or folder, usually went through several stages before reaching the press.

There was the creative concept. Then the production artwork. Then typesetting. Then image treatment. Then page assembly. Then film output. Then plate-making. And finally, printing.

Each step required specific knowledge. Very often, each step also required a different professional.

The result was a process that was slower, more manual, and more expensive.

When technology started to combine steps

Then came illustration software such as CorelDRAW, Adobe Illustrator, and FreeHand, along with desktop publishing tools such as PageMaker and QuarkXPress.

Parts of the process started to be combined. Work that previously required several hands began to happen in fewer steps.

Final artwork, text placement, image treatment, and page assembly started to move closer together inside the same digital workflow.

Then came further changes. Film preparation and film output became simpler. Later, film was no longer required in many processes, as computer-to-plate technology allowed digital files to be transferred directly to printing plates.

After that, for smaller print runs, even the plate itself started to disappear with the evolution of digital printing.

The production chain became shorter. The process became more technological and less manual. And the impact was significant.

What happened to the professionals

Some functions practically disappeared in the format in which they had existed. Highly specialized professionals saw part of their work absorbed by new tools and new workflows.

But that did not mean their knowledge lost value. Quite the opposite.

The professionals who were able to adapt, learn new tools, and combine their previous experience with new skills became extremely valuable. They understood the old process, but they were also able to operate in the new reality.

Not everyone made that transition. Some professionals left the industry and moved into different careers.

At the same time, lower costs, simplified processes, and faster production created room for new businesses to emerge. In some segments, professionals who had previously been employees became entrepreneurs.

New services appeared. New business models were created. New jobs were generated.

The market did not simply become smaller or larger. It became different.

Some activities lost relevance. Others emerged. Some skills were no longer enough. Others became essential.

The expectation around timelines

Another important change was the expectation around timelines.

In the past, producing a full-colour brochure could take 20 or 30 days. As technology evolved, that timeline dropped to a few days, then to the next day, and in some cases, to the same day.

With each technological leap, many people imagined that work would finally become calmer. The opposite happened.

The pressure increased. The market quickly adjusted to the new speed and stopped accepting the old timelines.

Productivity gains did not reduce expectations. They redefined the standard.

Lessons for project management in the age of AI

I see a few important lessons for project management in the age of AI.

The first lesson is that AI is already beginning to combine steps across many processes. Activities that once required several steps, several tools, or several people are starting to be executed in a more integrated way.

This does not necessarily mean that entire professions will disappear at once. But it does suggest that many tasks, responsibilities, and ways of working will be redesigned.

As happened in the printing industry, the accumulated knowledge of experienced professionals will continue to be valuable — as long as those professionals are able to adapt.

The opportunity is not only in learning a new tool. It is in combining business experience, professional judgment, process thinking, and practical use of new technologies.

For many professionals, this may mean developing skills adjacent to the ones they already have. For others, it may mean moving into new roles. And for some, it may even mean looking at the market not only as employees, but also as entrepreneurs.

With fewer technological barriers and less dependence on large operational structures, new opportunities may appear in markets that were previously harder to access.

The second lesson is about productivity. Many project managers may think that AI will finally reduce part of the operational work: status reports, meeting minutes, spreadsheet updates, action tracking, presentation preparation, and information organization. They are probably right.

But that does not necessarily mean this time will be used to work more calmly or only to focus on more strategic activities. History suggests that when productivity increases, market expectations tend to increase as well.

Today, a project manager may struggle to manage many projects at the same time with quality, especially when reporting, meetings, risks, dependencies, follow-ups, and stakeholder communication require so much manual effort.

With AI, automation, and digital agents, part of this operational work may become faster or partially automated. But the expectation around how many initiatives a project manager can handle may also increase significantly.

In other words, operational work may decrease, but responsibility, complexity, and pressure for results may increase.

The third lesson is about tool proficiency. For a long time, knowing how to use Excel, Google Sheets, PowerPoint, or project management tools such as Jira, MS Project, Confluence, or Azure DevOps has been considered an important skill for project managers.

Soon, it may no longer make much sense to ask whether a project manager knows how to use artificial intelligence tools. That may become basic.

The real differentiator will not be simply using AI. It will be knowing how to apply AI in practical ways to increase productivity, improve communication, anticipate risks, organize information, support decisions, and create better visibility for stakeholders.

This capability may exist at different levels: personal productivity, workflow automation, data analysis, content generation, communication preparation, decision support, and the use of digital agents.

For project managers, including experienced ones, developing this capability should be a priority.

The fourth lesson is that there is still some time to adapt — but not much. AI tools are still maturing. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, and other solutions are not exactly equivalent. In many cases, they are complementary.

Many companies are also still learning how to incorporate AI into their internal processes. Some are using one tool in a limited way. Others are still defining policies, security standards, governance models, and use cases.

This creates a window of opportunity. Those who start now will be better prepared for the moment when using AI is no longer a differentiator and becomes a basic expectation.

What will not change

Artificial intelligence should not eliminate the need for project management. But it will likely change how projects are planned, monitored, communicated, and delivered.

Technology may take over a growing portion of operational work. But it will still be necessary to define priorities, align stakeholders, manage risks, make decisions, resolve conflicts, interpret context, and lead people through change.

This may be the most important point.

AI can help produce meeting minutes. But someone still needs to understand what the meeting actually revealed.

AI can help generate a status report. But someone still needs to decide what message needs to be communicated, to whom, when, and with what level of transparency.

AI can help list risks. But someone still needs to interpret impact, probability, organizational context, team capacity, and decision appetite.

AI can help organize information. But someone still needs to lead people.

The better question

Maybe the most important question for project managers is not: "Will artificial intelligence replace my job?"

Maybe the better question is: "What part of my work will be automated, and what new capabilities do I need to develop to remain relevant?"

That is what happened in the printing industry. It was not only a change in tools. It was a change in markets, roles, expectations, and mindset.

And perhaps that is exactly what is beginning to happen now with project management.

For project managers, the challenge is not only to learn AI tools. The challenge is to understand how AI changes the value we are expected to bring.

What do you think will become more important for project managers as AI becomes part of everyday work?
