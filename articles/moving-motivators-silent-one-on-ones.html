Moving Motivators: Turning Silent One-on-Ones into Meaningful Conversations.

How a simple Management 3.0 tool helped turn silent, awkward one-on-ones into meaningful conversations about what truly motivates each person at work.

Management 3.0 Moving Motivators Leadership People Management One-on-One Meetings Employee Engagement

Originally published on LinkedIn on July 8, 2026

A one-on-one meeting with a direct report can serve many different purposes.

Sometimes it is used to give direction. Sometimes it is used to discuss project updates. Sometimes it is the right space to talk about performance, expectations, blockers, career development, or even difficult feedback.

The agenda can come from the manager, from the direct report, or ideally from both.

But in practice, that does not always happen.

As a manager, I learned that bringing my direct report's perspective to the table was not always easy. Some people are naturally more introverted. Others may feel shy, uncomfortable, or unsure about how to approach certain topics. In some cases, personal challenges outside the work environment can also affect how open someone feels during a conversation with their manager.

The result can be an awkward silence.

A meeting planned for thirty minutes may run out of content after ten. The direct report may answer briefly; the manager may try to fill the silence; and, very quickly, the one-on-one becomes a monologue.

Only the manager speaks.

The opportunity to listen, understand, and support the person is reduced.

During my Management 3.0 training, taught by Manoel Pimentel, I learned a simple and effective tool that helped me approach these situations differently: Moving Motivators.

Moving Motivators were developed by Jurgen Appelo as part of Management 3.0, and the tool immediately caught my attention because of its simplicity.

It gave me a way to transform a potentially short, silent, or uncomfortable meeting into a more meaningful conversation.

More importantly, it helped create a space where the direct report could reflect on what truly mattered to them at work — and where I, as a manager, could listen with more intention.

What Moving Motivators are

Moving Motivators is based on a set of ten cards, each one representing a different intrinsic motivator.

  • Relatedness — The need to have good social connections with people at work.
  • Curiosity — The need to explore, investigate, learn, and think about interesting things.
  • Acceptance — The need to feel accepted for who you are and for the work you do.
  • Goal — The need to see purpose and meaning reflected in your work.
  • Mastery — The need to be challenged in a way that stretches your competence without going beyond your abilities.
  • Status — The need to have your position, contribution, or role recognized by others.
  • Honour — The need to feel that your work reflects your personal values.
  • Power — The need to have enough influence over what happens around you.
  • Order — The need for structure, rules, clarity, and stability.
  • Freedom — The need to have independence in your work and responsibilities.

These motivators are neither good nor bad in themselves. They are also not the same for everyone.

For one person, Freedom may be at the top. For another, Order may be essential. Someone else may place Relatedness, Mastery, or Goal as their strongest driver.

That is exactly what makes the tool useful. It does not assume that everyone is motivated in the same way.

How to use it

The exercise starts with a simple step: the person organizes the cards from the most important motivator to the least important one.

There is no right or wrong order. The order simply reflects what matters more to that person.

The next step is to ask the person to move up the cards currently being positively supported by their work environment and move down the cards that are being negatively affected.

With that, the table is set.

Instead of asking a generic question such as "How are you feeling at work?", the manager can ask more focused and thoughtful questions:

  • Why is this motivator important to you?
  • Has this always been important, or is it more important now?
  • Do you feel this motivator is currently being supported in your work?
  • Is anything in the project, team, or organization affecting it positively or negatively?
  • Why is this motivator working well, or why is it not?

For example, a direct report may place Mastery very high because they enjoy being challenged and learning new things. But during the conversation, they may realize that their current assignments are too repetitive and no longer helping them grow.

Another person may place Order high because they need clarity, structure, and predictable rules to perform well. If they are working in a chaotic environment with unclear priorities, this may explain a frustration that had not yet been expressed directly.

Someone else may place Freedom at the top, but feel blocked by too many approvals, dependencies, or micromanagement.

This is where the tool becomes powerful. It helps transform vague discomfort into a clearer conversation.

Instead of assuming that someone is disengaged, resistant, unmotivated, or difficult, the manager can begin by understanding which motivators are being supported and which are being undermined.

Of course, Moving Motivators should not be used as a psychological test. It is not a diagnosis. It is not a label. And it should not become another corporate form to be completed and archived.

Its value is in the conversation it creates.

For me, it worked especially well in one-on-one meetings where the direct report struggled to bring topics to the table. The cards created a neutral starting point. The person was not forced to begin by exposing a problem, a frustration, or a complaint.

They were simply invited to reflect on what mattered to them.

That small change made the conversation safer, more natural, and more productive.

What I learned from using it

Using Moving Motivators in one-on-one meetings taught me a few important lessons.

The first one is that silence does not always mean the same thing. Some people are quiet by nature. Others are only silent in specific situations. Sometimes silence comes from personality. Sometimes it comes from lack of trust, discomfort, frustration, or even from something happening outside work.

As managers, we need to be careful not to interpret silence too quickly.

I also learned that high performers do not always complain. Many times, they simply act.

If something is bothering them, they may not raise the issue directly. They may not ask for help. They may not openly show dissatisfaction. Their next step might simply be to start looking for another job.

That is why creating space for honest conversations matters. A tool like Moving Motivators can help reveal early signals before disengagement becomes resignation.

I also learned that motivation is not only relevant for high performers.

Some people who are struggling may improve when we better understand what drives them. Low performance can have many causes: lack of clarity, lack of confidence, poor fit, lack of challenge, lack of autonomy, personal issues, or simply a mismatch between the person's motivators and the current work environment.

This does not mean that every performance issue can be solved through motivation alone. But it does mean that understanding motivation gives the manager better information before jumping to conclusions.

I also learned that some people need help beyond task management.

Sometimes, as a manager, you can go the extra mile personally. Other times, the best support is to connect the person with the right company structure, process, or resource.

The important point is not to assume that management is only about assigning work and checking progress. It is also about paying attention.

Finally, I learned something simple but powerful:

If you listen to your people, they are more likely to listen to you too.

Trust is not built only when the manager gives clear direction. It is also built when people feel heard before they are asked to perform, adapt, improve, or change.

For me, that was the biggest value of Moving Motivators. It helped me create better conversations. And better conversations often create better management.

Where to find the cards

I have my own physical deck of Moving Motivators cards, and I still like the tactile experience of using them in person.

However, in today's remote and hybrid work environment, a physical deck is not always practical.

Because of that, I created a simple virtual version that I can use during online one-on-one meetings: julio-gulias.github.io/moving-motivators

It is intentionally simple, but it works well as a conversation starter.

You can probably find more elaborate versions elsewhere, and I also recommend visiting the Management 3.0 website to learn more about the tool and its original context.

Final thought

A good one-on-one is not only a space for the manager to talk. It is a space for the manager to understand.

And sometimes, people need a little structure before they can open up.

Moving Motivators gave me that structure. Not to categorize people. Not to simplify their complexity. But to start better conversations about what matters to them.

And in management, that is often where real progress begins.
