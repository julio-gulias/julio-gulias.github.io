Job Search Scams: When Looking for Help Also Requires Caution.

A practical reflection on staying open to opportunities while validating people, companies, roles, and communication channels during a job search.

Job Search Scam Awareness Online Safety Recruitment Career Resilience Cybersecurity Awareness

Originally published on LinkedIn on July 4, 2025

Since I started a new job search in mid-March, I have already experienced at least seven or eight scam attempts.

Fortunately, my cybersecurity training and some instincts developed throughout my career helped me identify the red flags and stay protected.

But what concerns me the most is not only the number of attempts. It is the intention behind them.

Scammers try to take advantage of people who, because they are looking for a new opportunity, often expose themselves more than they normally would.

When we are searching for a job, we become more visible. We update our LinkedIn profiles. We reply to more messages. We accept more connection requests. We speak with people we do not know. We share our résumé. We open the door to conversations that, at another time, we might simply ignore.

That is part of the process.

The problem is that this openness also creates an opportunity for people with bad intentions.

Some present themselves as recruiters. Others appear as good Samaritans, offering help, connections, referrals, or access to opportunities.

And this is one of the most delicate parts of the situation.

When someone is looking for a job, they are also, in some way, looking for help.

A positive message can feel like a light at the end of the tunnel. A referral can feel like the missing chance. A person offering support can feel like exactly the lifeline that may make a difference.

But not every offer of help is truly help. Sometimes, what looks like an opportunity is only the beginning of a trap.

Some signs are easier to notice

Some approaches are so obvious that paying attention to a few details is enough to step away. For example:

  • HR or senior-level profiles with very few connections;
  • people claiming to represent a company but using a generic email account;
  • roles with salaries far above the market;
  • job descriptions that are vague, exaggerated, or too good to be true;
  • messages creating artificial urgency;
  • requests to quickly move away from verifiable platforms;
  • requests for personal information very early in the conversation;
  • recruitment processes with no real interview, no context, and no clarity.

In those cases, the best path is usually simple: stop the communication and move on.

The problem is that it is not always that clear. Some approaches are more elaborate. Some use the names of real companies. Some look professional at first. Some come from people who seem legitimate.

In those situations, it may take time to separate someone who is genuinely trying to help from someone trying to run a scam.

Caution is not paranoia

A job search requires openness. Security requires caution. The challenge is to balance both without closing the door to real opportunities and without exposing yourself too much to people with bad intentions.

That is why a few simple checks can help. Before moving forward in a conversation, it is worth validating:

  • whether the person actually works where they say they work;
  • whether the email address uses a corporate domain;
  • whether the role exists on the company's official website;
  • whether the person's profile has a coherent history, connections, and activity;
  • whether mutual connections can confirm the legitimacy of the approach;
  • whether the process follows a normal recruitment flow.

You do not need to treat everyone as suspicious. But you also do not need to trust before validating.

A simple message can help:

Thank you for reaching out. Since I have received a few fraudulent approaches during my job search, I am taking some basic validation steps before moving forward. Could you please share more details about the company, the role, and the best official channel to confirm this opportunity?

This message does not accuse anyone. It simply establishes a healthy boundary. And a serious person will likely understand.

Be careful with sensitive information too early

Some personal information may be required in a real recruitment process, especially after a formal offer or during onboarding. The problem is not the information itself. The problem is when it is requested too early, by an unverified person, through informal channels, or before there is a clear and legitimate process.

Before a role is validated, before a formal interview, or before an offer exists, it usually does not make sense to ask for:

  • full address;
  • personal documents;
  • banking information;
  • copies of identification documents;
  • date of birth;
  • family information;
  • references before there is a real process.

In the early stages, the most reasonable starting point is a conversation, context, LinkedIn, a résumé, and professional information.

If the process moves forward, more sensitive information should be requested through an official company channel, with a clear explanation of why it is needed, and ideally through a secure HR or onboarding system.

For job seekers, the rule is simple: do not share sensitive information before validating the person, the company, the role, and the communication channel.

Never pay to get a job

This point needs to be direct.

If someone asks for money to release a job opportunity, pay for mandatory training, buy equipment, cover administrative fees, pay for documents, or make any kind of deposit, the alarm should go off immediately.

Legitimate recruitment processes should not require candidates to pay for the process to move forward.

It is also important to be careful with proposals where someone sends money, cheques, transfers, or advance payments and then asks you to send part of it back, buy equipment from a specific vendor, or move funds on behalf of someone else.

When an opportunity starts involving money before there is a clear, formal, and verifiable employment relationship, it is time to stop and validate everything very carefully.

For those who want to help: transparency matters

This topic is not only important for job seekers. It is also important for people who genuinely want to help.

Many people really do help. They make introductions, share roles, refer candidates, offer conversations, review résumés, and open doors. That is extremely valuable.

But in the current environment, those who want to help also need to understand that the person on the other side may be more cautious than usual. And that is not ingratitude. It is security.

A transparent approach makes a big difference. Instead of a vague message like: "I have an opportunity for you. Send me your details."

A better message would be: "Hi, I saw your profile on LinkedIn and noticed that you are looking for a new opportunity. I work in technology and may be able to share your profile with a few people in my network. I am not representing a specific role at this moment. Please feel free to validate my profile before we continue the conversation."

Or, if the person is speaking on behalf of a company: "Hi, I am a recruiter at Company X and found your profile through LinkedIn. I would like to speak with you about a Program Manager role. You can also find the position on the company's official careers page. If you prefer, I can send the message from my corporate email so you can validate it more easily."

This kind of clarity reduces anxiety. It also prevents genuine help from looking suspicious.

Do not take validation questions personally

If someone who is looking for a job asks questions to validate who you are, try not to interpret that as coldness, personal distrust, or lack of gratitude.

Very often, it is simply a person trying to protect themselves.

Job seekers are already dealing with rejection, silence, anxiety, exposure, uncertainty, interviews, résumé adjustments, networking, and constant emotional ups and downs. Adding scams to this scenario makes everything even heavier.

So, if you want to help, help by creating trust as well. Explain who you are. Say how you found the person. Share context. Use verifiable channels. Do not ask for sensitive information too early. Give the person space to validate the approach.

That does not reduce the value of the help. In fact, it makes the help safer and more professional.

Looking for a job is already difficult enough

Looking for a job is emotionally demanding. There is hope, frustration, silence, interviews, networking, rejection, adjustment, and the need to keep starting again.

Nobody should also have to deal with people trying to exploit that moment. But that is the reality.

So, for those looking for a new opportunity, here is the reminder:

Accept help. Build connections. Talk to people. Stay open. But validate before you trust.

And for those who genuinely want to help, here is another reminder: be transparent. Offer context. Use verifiable channels. And do not be offended if the other person asks questions.

In times of so many scams, caution is not ingratitude. It is security.
